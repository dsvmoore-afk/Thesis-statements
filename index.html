What makes a Literary Thesis?

In English Literature, a thesis is not a summary of the plot. It is an arguable claim about the text's meaning, method, or context. A strong thesis answers the question: "How does this text work, and why does it matter?"

1. It is Arguable

A reasonable person could disagree with you. It is not a statement of fact.

2. It is Specific

It avoids vague universals like "society" or "human nature" and focuses on the text.

3. It is Complex

It often acknowledges tension, irony, or contradiction within the work.

Fixing Weak Thesis Statements

Click the buttons to see how a weak observation transforms into a strong literary argument.

Bad: Victor Frankenstein messes with nature and it goes wrong.

Why: This is a plot summary. It's a fact, not an argument.

Better: By juxtaposing the sublimity of the Alps with Victor’s grotesque laboratory, Shelley argues that nature is not merely a resource to be exploited, but a moral force that punishes scientific hubris.

Why: It identifies a method (juxtaposition of settings) and an argument (nature as moral force).

Bad: Hamlet pretends to be crazy to trick his uncle.

Why: Obvious observation. No analysis.

Better: While Hamlet initially feigns madness as a political strategy, Shakespeare uses the ambiguity of his soliloquies to suggest that the performance of insanity eventually erodes the boundary between the actor and the true self.

Why: It acknowledges complexity (tension between feigned vs. real) and focuses on the soliloquies (method).

Bad: Austen shows that people should marry for love, not money.

Why: A cliché moral lesson. Too simple.

Better: Through the ironic narrative voice, Austen critiques the economic necessity of marriage for 19th-century women, suggesting that while love is ideal, financial pragmatism is a survival mechanism rather than a moral failing.

Why: It analyzes tone (irony) and nuances the argument (pragmatism vs. romance).

The "So What?" Checklist

Before submitting your thesis, ask these three questions. If the answer is "No," revise.

  • Can I point to the text? Does your argument rely on close reading of specific passages, or general generalizations about life?
  • Is the opposite defensible? If you say "War is bad in this poem," no one disagrees. If you say "The poem portrays war as a necessary evil," that is debatable.
  • Does it analyze form? Do you mention how the text is written (imagery, meter, structure, tone) or just what happens?
